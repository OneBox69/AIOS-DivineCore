## 1. What Is a Lead Magnet (Hormozi Definition)

> "A lead magnet is a complete solution to a narrow problem. It's typically a lower-cost or free offer to see who is interested in your stuff. And, once solved, it reveals another problem solved by your core offer."
> — Alex Hormozi, 100 Million Leads (p.48)

**The salty pretzels analogy:**
The pretzels solve the narrow problem of hunger. They also create thirst — which is solved by a drink you pay for. The pretzel has a cost, but the drink revenue covers it and nets profit. Your lead magnet works the same way.

**Core principle:**
- A lead magnet solves one problem completely AND for free (or very cheap)
- Solving that problem reveals a bigger problem
- That bigger problem is solved by your core (paid) offer
- A person who pays with their time now is more likely to pay with money later

## 2. Hormozi's 7 Steps to an Effective Lead Magnet

**Step 1 — Pick the narrow problem and who to solve it for**
Use the Problem-Solution Cycle: every solution reveals a new problem. Pick a narrow problem that, when solved, naturally surfaces the bigger problem your core offer addresses.

**Step 2 — Figure out how to solve it (3 types)**

### Type 1: Reveal Their Problem (Diagnosis)
Works best when the problem gets worse the longer they wait and the prospect doesn't fully know the extent of it.
- Website speed test → shows how much revenue they lose from slow load times
- Posture analysis → shows the gap between current posture and pain-free life
- Termite inspection → either they have termites (sell removal) or they don't (sell prevention)

### Type 2: Samples and Trials
Full but brief access to your core offer. Works best when your core offer solves a recurring problem.
- Hook them to your faster server → they see results → must keep paying to keep access
- Free chiropractic adjustment → they feel relief → need more to maintain it
- Consumable samples (food, cosmetics) → recurring use solves recurring problem

### Type 3: One Step of a Multi-Step Process
Give one valuable step free, charge for the rest. Works best when your core offer solves a complex, multi-step problem.
- This book (100M Leads) → solves "not enough leads" → reveals "can't scale alone" → Hormozi's investment offer
- Free wood sealant coat → explains it needs 3 coats for full protection → sells the bundle
- Free finance course → valuable on its own → reveals the time cost of DIY → sells financial services

**Step 3 — Choose how to deliver it (4 methods)**

| Method | What it is | Example |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| Software | A tool that does a job for them | Spreadsheet, calculator, dashboard |
| Information | Something they can learn from | Mini course, guide, checklist, report |
| Services | You do work for free | Audit, adjustment, analysis, transformation |
| Physical product | Something they can hold | Book, sample, kit |

With 3 types × 4 delivery methods = up to 12 lead magnet variants per narrow problem.

**Step 4 — Test the name (headline)**
> "Five times more people read your headline than any other part of your promotion."
> — David Ogilvy (cited by Hormozi)

Test in this order:
1. Headline (most important — can 2x, 3x, or 10x engagement alone)
2. Image(s)
3. Subheadline

**Step 5 — Make it easy to consume**
If they don't consume it, they don't feel the value. Format matters as much as content.

**Step 6 — Make it darn good**
> "Make your lead magnets as good as your paid stuff."

Grand Slam Offer rules apply to free stuff just as much as paid offers. The business that provides the most value wins.

**Step 7 — Make it easy for them to tell you they want more**
The lead magnet ends with a natural bridge to the next step. Don't leave them hanging — give them a clear, easy path to the core offer.

## 3. The Economics (Why Lead Magnets Beat Direct Offers)

Hormozi's example:
- Direct offer: spend $10,000, get 10 customers = $1,000 cost per customer
- With lead magnet (costs $25 to deliver): spend $1,000 + $250 delivery = still ~10 customers acquired
- But the lead magnet attracts 10x more people who engage = lower cost per customer
- Even if lead magnet costs money to deliver — the conversion improvement more than covers it

**Key insight:** When you nail the lead magnet, you can provide FAR more value at FAR lower cost than competitors who go straight for the sale.

---

## 4. Critical Rules (Hormozi)

1. **It must be valuable enough to charge for** — if you wouldn't pay for it, neither will they engage with it
2. **After consuming it, they should want more** — if the lead magnet doesn't create desire for the core offer, it's the wrong magnet
3. **It must be specific to a narrow problem** — broad solutions make poor lead magnets
4. **Make as many versions as you can and rotate them** — you won't know which works best until you test
5. **Solve the narrow problem completely** — don't half-deliver and then upsell. Fully solve it. The next problem will surface naturally.

## 5. B2B Cold Email Lead Magnet Research (Web)

### What converts in B2B cold email (2025 data)
- Interactive tools and audits: 20–30% conversion rate
- Checklists and templates: 20–30%
- Reports and guides: 15–20%
- Generic "let's hop on a call" CTA: typically <2%

### Why service-based lead magnets win in cold outreach
When you do actual work (audit, analysis, research) and show the OUTPUT in or attached to the cold email:
- Proves you're not mass-blasting — you genuinely researched them
- Creates reciprocity — they received something before being asked for anything
- Lowers the ask dramatically — "want me to send the full report?" vs. "want a sales call?"
- The reply opens a real conversation, not a sales call ambush

### The personalization multiplier
The more the lead magnet references THEIR specific business (their numbers, their name, their specific gaps), the higher the conversion. Generic lead magnets get ignored. Personalized ones get forwarded internally.

### Best CTA formats in B2B cold email
| CTA type | Friction level | Typical reply rate |
|----------|---------------|-------------------|
| "Want a 30-min call?" | High | Low |
| "Would this be useful?" | Medium | Medium |
| "Want me to send it over?" | Low | High |
| "Reply YES and I'll send it" | Very Low | Highest |

## 6. Hook Frameworks for Cold Email

### The AIDA Framework
- **A**ttention — grab them in the first line with something specific to them
- **I**nterest — show you understand their situation
- **D**esire — make them want the outcome
- **A**ction — one clear, low-friction ask

### The "I noticed X" hook
Leads with a specific observation about their business. Not a compliment — an observation that implies you did research.
- Weak: "I came across your website and really liked it"
- Strong: "I noticed you're running Google Ads but your contact form is only checked during business hours"

### The "Here's what I found" hook
You lead with a result, not a question. You've already done the work. They just decide if they want it.
- "Ran a quick audit of [company]. Found 3 gaps. Putting it in a report — want me to send it over?"

### The Benchmark hook
You compare them to an industry standard. Works because everyone wants to know how they rank.
- "Your average Google review response time is 14 days. Top-performing clinics in your area respond in under 2."

### The Dollar Amount hook
You quantify the pain in dollars. Not "you might be losing leads" but "estimated $X/month."
- "Based on your ad spend and current response time, you're likely losing $1,200–$2,400/month in missed inquiries."

## 7. What Makes a Lead Magnet Fail

- Too broad — tries to solve everything, solves nothing well
- Too generic — could apply to any business, feels like a template
- No bridge to core offer — solves the problem completely but leaves no reason to buy
- Too much friction to receive — requires a long form, booking a call, signing up, etc.
- Not specific enough numbers — vague pain ("you might be losing leads") vs. specific pain ("$1,800/month estimate")
- The CTA asks for too much — never ask for a call as the first step from a cold email
