## Core principles

- **Consult, don't pitch.** Most of the call is asking, not selling. If you're talking more than 40% of the time, recalibrate.
- **Don't close on the call.** Closing live makes them uncomfortable and makes you improvise pricing. Always commit to a written proposal/audit doc within 24h.
- **Take temperature checks throughout.** *"Does that make sense?"* / *"Is this what you were looking for?"* / *"Anything I'm missing?"* lets you course-correct in real time.
- **Let them feel in control.** Despite running the call, ask permission at every transition (*"Sound good?"* / *"Cool with you?"*). Documented sales technique — they'll feel like they chose every step.
- **If you nail the why-triad, 90% of the call's work is done.** Everything after is confirming what you already learned.

## Structure

| Minutes | Section | Goal |
|---------|---------|------|
| 0-5     | Rapport + meeting-notes offer | Warm them up, transition |
| 5-8     | Set the schedule + decision authority | Frame the call, surface approval path |
| 8-30    | Diagnosis (why-triad → goals → pipeline → numbers → function deep-dive) | Find the highest-leverage gap |
| 30-37   | Frame the offer (with temperature checks) | Pilot + retainer |
| 37-42   | Risk reversal + objections | Address hesitation |
| 42-45   | No close — commit to proposal, lock follow-up, "anything else?" | Lock next step |

## 0–5 · Rapport + meeting-notes offer

Brief small talk — load the relevant persona file ([`mayank.md`](../identity/mayank.md) or [`pang.md`](../identity/pang.md)) for the actual content; this isn't a script. Keep it light, get them laughing if you can, humanize early. *"You're not the only one anxious on this call by the way"* lands well.

Once they're warmed up, transition out:

> *"Alright, you ready to jump in? Also — I run an AI summarizer on these calls and can share the notes with you after we're done, so neither of us has to write anything. Cool with you?"*

Two wins: small trust signal (you're organized + transparent) and they don't have to take notes, so they can actually engage.

## 5–8 · Set the schedule + decision authority

This is the most underused move in discovery calls. Telling them how the call goes upfront lowers anxiety, frames you as someone who's done this before, and gives you permission to drive.

> *"I do a lot of these calls day-to-day, here's how they normally go:*
>
> *First — I just want to learn why you reached out. You probably get 20 or 30 of these messages a week, so there was a reason you booked. I want to understand that.*
>
> *Then I'll get some background on the business — chat numbers, where you guys are at today. I'll share some numbers on my end too so it's not a one-way thing.*
>
> *Finally — if there's overlap, if I think I can help you, I'll tell you exactly how, and we'll cover next steps.*
>
> *Sound good?"*

Then immediately, before diagnosis:

> *"One quick logistics thing first — if by the end of the call we agree this is a fit, what does it take on your side to greenlight a pilot? Is that your call, or does someone else need to weigh in?"*

The phrase *"by the end of the call"* plants the close without pushing.

### Decision authority — three branches

#### Branch A — "My call, but I'd loop in [co-founder]."
Fine. Keep going. End-of-call prep:
> *"Makes sense. Let's make sure I give you everything you'd need to share with [co-founder] so they have the full picture too."*

#### Branch B — "Actually [someone else] makes the call."
Don't pretend you can still close. Two options, let them pick:
> *"Got it — I want to make sure I'm not wasting your time or [decision-maker]'s. Two options: either we wrap in 20 min on the basics and book a proper call with [decision-maker] on, or we keep going and I'll structure the audit doc tomorrow specifically so you can walk them through it. Which works better?"*

Most will say "let's keep going." If so — write the audit doc *for the absent decision-maker*, not for the person on the call.

#### Branch C — Vague / "I think so?"
Push gently with a past-behavior question:
> *"Just so I know how to structure next steps — when you've made decisions like this in the past, who else has been involved?"*

Past behavior is the cleanest signal.

## 8–30 · Diagnosis

Five-stage sequence. Don't skip ahead. The order matters — each stage primes the next.

### A · The why-triad (5 min) — *why us, why this, why now*

The single most important diagnostic block on the call. Three angles on the same question:

> *"So — why did you decide to book in the first place? You didn't have to. What compelled you?"*

Let them talk. They'll surface their pain, their frustrations, the language they use about their own problem. Take notes — this is the language you use in the proposal.

Then:
> *"Have you tried solving this before? Other agency, tool, hire? What did you spend, and what was the outcome?"*

This sets you apart from whoever failed before — and gives you a budget anchor without asking.

Then:
> *"And why now? Why didn't you do this 3 weeks ago, or push it to next quarter?"*

Surfaces urgency. If they shrug, they're not ready — adjust your urgency in the offer accordingly. If they have a real reason (hiring freeze, Q4 push, returns season hitting), you've got a deadline you can scope around.

### B · Goals & success definition (3 min)

> *"What are you trying to do with the business this year? What's the goal?"*
>
> *"And if we did this perfectly — what does success look like 90 days from now? How would you know it worked?"*

The second question is critical. **You can't deliver a pilot if you can't define success up front.** This is also where the risk-reversal metric comes from — whatever they say here is what you guarantee against in the proposal.

### C · Pipeline mapping (5 min)

> *"All right — before I propose anything, mind walking me through how the business actually runs today? What's the very first touch point a customer has with you? Then what happens next?"*

Map their full customer journey: ad → site → cart → CS → fulfilment → returns → repurchase. As they describe it, you'll spot the gaps yourself. Genuinely impressive, leave-it-on-the-shelf type observations:

> *"Wow — so every return ticket goes through [employee] manually? That must be eating hours a week."*

Now they're nodding along to your diagnosis. This is the quiet version of selling.

### D · Numbers anchor (3 min)

The cleanest way to get revenue and team data without it feeling like an interrogation: anchor with a peer reference.

> *"How big's the team, 4 or 5 people? Cool — I was working with another brand about your size, they were doing around £[X]m/year. You guys somewhere in that range?"*

The peer reference does three things at once:
1. They tell you their numbers comfortably (you went first, sort of)
2. You're positioned as someone who works with their peers, not above or below
3. It's a humble flex without being one

Then:
> *"How long has the brand been around? Are you growing quickly?"*

Tenure + growth → tells you whether they're in *catch up to ops chaos* mode (fast-growing, drowning) or *optimize what we have* mode (mature, plateau). Different sales angle for each.

### E · Function deep-dive (5–7 min)

By now the function should be obvious — either from their pre-call answer, the why-triad, or the pipeline map. Drill in:

- *"Who handles [function] today? How many hours/week between them?"*
- *"Roughly what does that person cost — junior, mid, senior, founder?"*
- **VALUE-ANCHOR (the most important question on the call):**
  *"If [that person] had 10 hours back this week, what would they actually do with it?"*
- *"And what would moving the needle on this be worth to you over the next quarter?"*

If they're stuck on which function to pick: default to **the biggest gap from your audit**, not "marketing because we know marketing." Diagnosis-led, not pitch-led.

> *"From what I saw this morning, [specific function] looks like where the biggest leverage is — does that match how you feel internally?"*

## 30–37 · Frame the offer

Bridge into the offer with the natural transition:

> *"Alright — that's everything I needed. While you were talking, a couple of things jumped out to me. To be clear, I've done very similar things for other clients, never really bigger than you, around the same size. Mind if I run you through what I'd recommend?"*

Pause. Let them say yes. Then:

> *"Based on what you're telling me and what I saw this morning, the highest-leverage place to start is [specific function]. The frame is simple: **your business, running on AI**. Here's how we'd run it:*
>
> ***Weeks 1-2: Context.*** *We learn your business — products, voice, SOPs, decisions, why you do things the way you do. This becomes the AI brain.*
>
> *(temp check: "Does that make sense so far?")*
>
> ***Weeks 3-4: Data.*** *We connect the brain to your numbers (Shopify, Klaviyo, Gorgias, whatever you actually use). Now it sees what's happening in real time.*
>
> ***Weeks 5-7: Build.*** *We ship one specific workflow tied to [their pain]. Live, monitored, your team uses it daily.*
>
> ***Week 8: Verify.*** *Delivery test against what we agreed at kickoff. That's when payment happens.*
>
> *By week 8 you have a brain that's learned your business and a workflow taking real work off the team. From there, every new workflow we add runs cheaper and faster because the brain is already trained.*
>
> *(temp check: "Is this the kind of thing you were imagining? Anything I'm missing?")*
>
> *On pricing, **pilot is around £2.5k**, fixed at kickoff. Per project, so it can flex up or down a bit depending on scope. That's it for the pilot."*

Then transition straight into the retainer (don't pause, don't let them ask):

> *"After the pilot, you transition into our **Foundation retainer at around £2.5k/month**. That covers maintaining your context layer as the business changes (new products, new SOPs, voice changes), plus monitoring on the live workflows and a weekly 30-min check-in. Up to 2 active workflows included.*
>
> *Each new workflow we ship after that adds £500/month. So a brand running 5 active workflows is at £3.5k/month.*
>
> *The retainer is the lock-in not because of contracts — there isn't one — but because if you stop maintaining the context layer, the system gets stale fast. We're keeping your operating brain current as the business evolves."*

**Default-on, opt-out.** The retainer is part of the pilot offer, not a separate sale.

### Temperature-check phrases to weave in
- *"Does that make sense?"*
- *"Is this what you were looking for?"*
- *"Anything I'm missing?"*
- *"How does that sit with you?"*

If they say *"how would you actually build it?"* — don't improvise the technical scope live. Defer to the proposal:

> *"Glad you asked — the system you're describing is similar to one I've built before, [brief 1-line reference]. Rather than guess on the call, let me scope it properly tonight and put the specifics in the proposal tomorrow."*

## 37–42 · Risk reversal + objections

The guarantee is locked. Two variants — pick one before the call based on the prospect (TYPE B is the default; only use TYPE A when all 4 signals from [`guarantee.md`](guarantee.md) §02 are present).

**TYPE B (functions delivered, default):**

> *"We're confident enough in this that we put skin in the game. The deal: by the end of week 8, the system performs the functions we agree on at kickoff. You don't pay me anything upfront — you only pay when it's delivered and we've verified it works. If a function in the spec is missing or doesn't work, you don't pay anything, and I pay you $500 out of my own pocket for the time you committed. Real measurement, not vibes."*

**TYPE A (hours saved):**

> *"We're confident enough in this that we put skin in the game. The deal: at least **10 hours per week back** for your team by the end of month 2. We log the baseline before we start, log the post-launch state at month 2, and if hours saved isn't 10+/week, you don't pay anything, and I pay you $500 out of my own pocket for the time you committed. Real measurement, not vibes."*

If they push: *"why hours and not, say, response time or revenue?"*

> *"Hours is the cleanest signal across any workflow. Response time matters for CS but doesn't apply to content; revenue impact takes 6 months to attribute cleanly. Hours saved hits within weeks, applies everywhere, and compounds visibly as we add workflows. Other metrics (response time, throughput, conversion) we still track and report, but the guarantee is hours."*

You can commit to the guarantee on the call. The metric is universal within each variant.

### Common objections to be ready for

- **"It's expensive."** → *"Compared to what? You mentioned [employee] is spending [X hours/week] on this — what's that costing you in salary alone over a year?"*
- **"We'd want to wait until [later quarter]."** → *"Totally fine. The thing to flag — every week you wait is another week of [pain]. Want to at least lock the audit doc so you can decide with full information?"*
- **"How is this different from [tool / agency they mentioned]?"** → Don't trash the alternative. *"[Tool] does [narrow thing] well. What we do is different — we're building the operating brain that all of those tools eventually plug into. Without it, every tool stays a silo."*

## 42–45 · No close — commit to proposal + lock follow-up

**Don't try to close on the call.** Pricing and the guarantee are locked, but the *specific workflow* and how we'd build it isn't — that's what the audit doc commits in writing. Live closes feel high-pressure and rush the scope. Stay deliberate:

> *"Here's what I'd like to do — let me scope the specifics properly tonight. I'll check the integrations involved, sketch how the workflow would actually run, and put it into a 1-page audit doc with three ranked opportunities and the workflow we'd start with. Pricing and the guarantee you already have. You'll get the doc by [tomorrow EOD].*
>
> *Take a day with it, loop in [co-founder / decision-maker], and let's reconnect [specific day, 2-3 days out] to talk through it. Cool?"*

**Lock the follow-up call on the discovery call itself.** Specific time, calendar invite sent before you hang up. Half of pipeline death happens because nobody booked the next step.

End naturally — don't make the close feel like the close:

> *"Anything else you want to talk about or ask before we wrap?"*

This last question often surfaces the real objection they were holding back. Listen carefully — whatever they say goes into the proposal.
