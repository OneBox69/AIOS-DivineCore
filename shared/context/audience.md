# Audience — Who DivineSide writes for

> Voice-shaped audience definition. Every writing agent should load this file before producing content — the person on the receiving end determines tone, examples, and which arguments land.

## Primary audience

**Founders, entrepreneurs, and operators running real businesses (~$50k–$10M ARR) who suspect AI should be doing more than they've figured out how to make it do.**

Not technical leadership at FAANG. Not students. Not "AI enthusiasts" who don't run anything. The buyer is someone who *operates* — they have payroll to make, customers to keep happy, a function in their business that's eating their week.

## Sub-segments

### 1. The agency owner / consultant
- Runs a marketing, sales, or ops agency.
- 5–30 people. Drowning in execution work.
- Has tried "AI tools" — Zapier, ChatGPT, maybe Make. Got 10% leverage, hit a wall.
- Wants: their agency to actually scale without 10x hiring.
- Doesn't trust most AI agency pitches because most are chatbot-shaped.

### 2. The eCom / DTC founder
- $500k–$5M ARR. Lean team.
- Currently relying on a VA or Upwork contractor for content, sales follow-ups, customer ops.
- Wants: an autonomous system handling content creation OR sales pipeline OR customer ops, freeing the founder for product/strategy.
- Skeptical buyer — has been burned by overpromising agencies.

### 3. The solo operator / indie founder
- Building a SaaS, info product, or service business alone.
- Reads Twitter/X, watches YouTube founders, follows the build-in-public scene.
- Wants: leverage. Wants to look at someone running a business mostly via systems and ask "how."
- High content engagement, lower conversion-to-build (smaller wallet) — but the ones who DO convert become long-term advocates.

### 4. The corporate operator (future)
- Director / VP at a 200–2000 person company. Owns a function (sales ops, content, customer success).
- Currently shopping for "AI tools" because that's what their procurement allows.
- Doesn't yet have language for "AI OS" — but recognises the value when shown.
- Stage 2 (GaaS) buyer. Not the primary target today.

## What they all share

- **Operator mindset.** They DO the work, they don't just talk about it.
- **Skepticism toward AI hype.** They've heard "AI will change your business" 200 times. They want to see something working.
- **Time-poor.** Read on phones, between meetings. Hooks have to land in 2 seconds.
- **Pattern-matching.** They've seen the consultant playbook. They notice when copy is templated.
- **Respect for specifics.** Numbers, named tools, real screenshots, real outcomes. Vagueness gets them off the page.

## What they DON'T want

- Inspirational quotes about "the future of AI."
- Generic "5 prompts that will transform your business."
- Long-winded LinkedIn essays where the point is in paragraph 7.
- Pitches that don't name what's being sold.
- Anything that smells like an MBA wrote it.

## How to write to them

| When you're writing | Default to |
|---------------------|------------|
| **Hooks** | A specific claim or a real number. Not curiosity gaps. |
| **Examples** | Real builds, real client names (where allowed), real failures. |
| **Authority** | Show the work. Architecture diagrams beat credentials. |
| **CTA** | Low-friction first step. DM, reply, comment — not "book a 30-minute discovery call." |
| **Length** | Short by default. Earn each additional paragraph. |

## What converts them

In rough order of effectiveness, today:

1. **Watching us build something they recognise as hard.** Architecture posts, system breakdowns, "here's how Pulse works."
2. **Specific client outcomes.** "We replaced X for client Y, saved Z hours/week."
3. **Mayank's personal POV.** Strong takes, named opinions, things other agencies are too cautious to say.
4. **Behind-the-scenes failure stories.** What broke, what we changed, what we learned.

What does NOT convert them (yet):

- Lead magnets / freebies / "download my framework."
- "Book a strategy call" cold.
- Generic AI commentary.

## Update cadence

This file should evolve as we close deals and learn what actually buys. Every closed client should add a line — what segment they fell in, what content they came from, what objection they had. Over time this becomes the most valuable file in the repo.
