# Outbound Copywriting Playbook

> **Cross-channel tactical playbook for cold outbound copy.** Cold email, LinkedIn DM, Instagram DM, X DM, SMS, even cold call openers. The principles are the same: convince a stranger with zero pre-established trust to take an action.
>
> **Why outbound is different from other copywriting:** every other discipline assumes opt-in (newsletter signup, form fill, warm intro). Outbound has none of that. The reader did not ask for the message. So every line has to do work that opt-in copy gets for free.

## 01 · The Seven Principles of Yes

These are the psychological levers behind why a stranger says yes to a message. Most are from Cialdini's *Influence*, plus voluntary disclosure (FBI rapport tactic) and a few practitioner additions. Every cold message should bake in **at least three** of these. Recognize the patterns; you do not have to memorize the labels.

### 1. Give First

When you provide something the reader assigns value to, you create a sense of obligation that lowers their resistance and disarms skepticism. Restaurants give you mints before the bill. Costco hands out samples (2-3% conversion off a free taste). Same mechanism.

In outbound this means **opening with a useful observation, audit, or insight that you give away with no immediate ask attached**. The ask is inferred; you do not need to make it explicit.

The give does not have to be objectively valuable. It only has to be valuable *to the reader*.

- DO: *"Noticed your booking page is loading the Stripe SDK twice. That is probably costing you 1.5s of TTI on mobile and is the easiest 5-minute fix on the page. Screenshot below."*
- DON'T: *"I have helped many companies like yours improve performance. Want to hop on a call?"*

The first one gives. The second one asks. The first one earns the next message.

### 2. Micro-Commitments

You cannot ask a stranger for $4,000 cold. You can ask them to watch a 60-second Loom. If they watch the Loom, you can ask for a 15-minute call. If they take the call, you can send a proposal. Each yes makes the next yes easier.

The reverse is also true: a single oversized ask kills the entire ladder. The whole game of outbound is **shrinking the next ask until the reader cannot reasonably say no**.

The classic study: every additional small "yes" before the real ask raises the probability of yes on the real ask. The brain leaks; this is one of the leaks you exploit.

- DO: *"If even after 60 seconds you don't think it's useful, tell me to screw off and I won't message again."* (Tiny ask + permission to bail = much higher yes rate.)
- DON'T: *"Book a 30-minute discovery call so I can walk you through our process."* (Zero context, big ask, generic outcome.)

### 3. Social Proof

Humans are herd-consensus animals. Before saying yes to anything uncertain, we look around to see who else has already said yes. Cold outbound is max-uncertainty, so social proof matters more here than almost anywhere else.

Three rules:

- **Show others taking the action**, not just naming them.
- **Use specific numbers**, not vague claims. *"Generated $112,482 last week for an HVAC business in Tennessee"* beats *"we get great results."*
- **Match the reference group**. If you are pitching a B2B SaaS, cite a B2B SaaS case study. A dog-walker case study cited at a SaaS company actively hurts you. Maximize Venn-diagram overlap between the cited reference and the prospect's ICP.

- DO: *"Just finished a build for a UK beauty brand doing £2M annually, same size as you. Same exact pipeline. Want me to walk you through the diff?"*
- DON'T: *"We have helped many businesses scale."*

### 4. Authority

Demonstrate hyper-relevant expertise through credentials, accomplishments, or just confident framing. The doctor signs *Dr. X, MD* because the title shifts how seriously the reader takes the next sentence.

**Match the credential to the audience.** A behavioral neuroscience credential lands badly in a blue-collar shop. *"I'm a Google Partner"* lands well there. The credential has to mean something *to them*. Authority is in the eye of the receiver, not the giver.

In DivineSide's case the working authority anchors are: AIOS framing, named clients, the 8-hours-back-by-week-4 guarantee, real workflows we have shipped. Use whichever the reader will recognize.

- DO: *"Built the proposal pipeline that powers our Upwork apps. ~80% of replies turn into intro calls. Same architecture, your business."*
- DON'T: *"We are a leading AI agency."* (Empty. Means nothing to the reader.)

Also signal authority through **confidence in tone**. Hedging every sentence reads as low-status.

- DO: *"This will save you 8+ hours a week. I am sure of it because I have shipped four of these already."*
- DON'T: *"I think we might be able to maybe help you possibly save some time."*

### 5. Rapport

Find shared context. Cultural, ethnic, professional, geographic, dog breed. Anything specific. The more specific, the more the reader believes you actually noticed them.

Two layers:

- **Explicit rapport.** The literal mention. *"Saw you both went to UCLA. My cousin was there 2018-2022."*
- **Implicit rapport.** Mirroring the *form* of their writing. Match their punctuation, message length, casing, tone. If the prospect's whole LinkedIn voice is lowercase no-punctuation tech-Twitter, your DM should be lowercase no-punctuation. If they write in long crisp paragraphs, do the same.

Implicit rapport is the higher-leverage move and the one most outbound senders ignore. SF VCs in 2022 received heavy lowercase outbound for a reason. It signals *"I am one of you."*

### 6. Voluntary Disclosure of Information

Used by the FBI to build rapport with sources, and now in good cold outbound. **When you voluntarily reveal something about yourself, the reader trusts you more** because you have just spent social capital on them without being asked.

The disclosure does not have to be huge. It just has to be specific and personal-feeling.

- *"Love your channel, very no-BS. Helped me get started in management consulting."* (The "got me started in management consulting" is the disclosure. It barely matters whether it's true. The reader feels you told them something about you.)
- *"My GF says I listen to you more than I listen to her, lol."*
- *"Was standing on the side of the street calling an Uber when I saw your post."*

Used right, voluntary disclosure converts the reader from "stranger evaluating a sales pitch" into "person hearing from another person."

### 7. Scarcity

Limit availability or attach a real deadline. The constraint *must be real* or the reader's BS detector trips and you lose more than you gain.

Sources of real scarcity that work for DivineSide:

- **Capacity.** *"I have one pilot slot left this month before I close intake until July."*
- **Pricing window.** *"Proposal numbers are good through Friday; after that I have to re-quote at the new rate."*
- **Personal schedule.** *"I am only running calls on Tuesday and Thursday this week, then I am off-grid for a build sprint."*

**Admitting your own limit (capacity, schedule conflicts) actually adds credibility** because it concedes you are not infinitely available. Most senders avoid this. It is the most underused principle in the seven.

### 8. Shared Identity

Stronger than rapport. Rapport is "we have something in common." Identity is "we are the same kind of person." Operator-to-operator. Founder-to-founder. Builder-to-builder.

For DivineSide this is usually: *founder who has shipped real things talking to another founder who has shipped real things*. Not consultant-to-client. Not vendor-to-buyer.

Activate it through:

- In-group language (the way that audience actually talks about their work)
- Shared struggle (we have both lived through the thing you are dealing with)
- Form-mirroring (lowercase, brevity, cadence, whatever signals "I am one of you")

If the prospect can read your message and think *"this person gets it"* before they ever read your offer, you have already won.

## 02 · Three Components of a Successful Outbound Campaign

Before any single message, every campaign needs three things settled. Skip any of these and the writing will not save you.

### A. A Clear Goal. One Sentence

If you cannot describe the goal of the campaign in one sentence, you are not ready to write it. Pick **one** action you want the reader to take after reading the message:

| Goal | When to use |
|------|-------------|
| **Reply** | Conversational opener; you want them to engage so you can qualify in the reply |
| **Watch a Loom / asset** | You have something genuinely useful to show; the asset does the convincing |
| **Book a call** | High-intent + you can offer specific times in the message itself |
| **Buy** | Rare in cold; only for low-ticket products with one-click checkout. The "holy grail" but almost never used in B2B. |

**Pick one.** Do not stack goals. Each additional goal cuts conversion on the primary one.

The phrasing of the message changes completely depending on which one you picked. A reply-goal message ends with a question. A book-a-call-goal message ends with two specific time slots. A watch-the-Loom-goal message ends with the literal link.

**Single message must do the whole job.** Treat each individual message as a self-contained campaign. You will add follow-ups later, but the first message should be able to convert on its own if every follow-up was deleted.

### B. The Frame. Write Like a Human

The single biggest unlock in outbound copy is making the message *feel one-to-one*, even when it is being sent to 500 people via template.

**Tests for frame:**

- **Text message test.** If a friend looked over your shoulder while you typed this, would they think it was a personal message or a mass email? Optimize for personal. Look at how you actually text people you have a strong-trust low-formality relationship with. That is your tone target.
- **Person-to-person, never company-to-prospect.** Use *"I,"* not *"we."* Use *"you,"* not *"your team."*
- **Length.** Short. Aim for under 90 words on email. Under 60 on DM. Cut everything that is not load-bearing.
- **Casual register.** Slightly imperfect grammar, occasional contractions, sentence fragments. Real humans do not write like LinkedIn bios.

**Kill these corporate signals:**

- *"Hope this finds you well"* / *"I hope you are doing well"* / *"Trust you are well"*
- *"Reaching out to you regarding..."*
- Multi-line signature blocks with title, company, phone, address
- *"We"* when you mean *"I"*
- *"At [Company], we believe..."*
- Calendly link without a one-line reason it is in the message
- Anything that sounds like a press release

**Tactical authenticity moves:**

- Add `Sent from my iPhone` to the bottom of email outbound. The mobile signal reads as "this person typed this on the go," which is the opposite of "this is a campaign."
- Lowercase the subject line.
- Leave a typo or two in. Not in the personalization line. In the body or the sign-off, where a real human would have moved on too fast to notice. Source's own example: signing off *"GVE me a shout. Thanks, Mikuel"* (typo in the verb).
- Sign with first name only. No title.

### C. Iteration. Treat the Campaign Like a Science Experiment

Almost no campaign one-shots. Realistic flow:

1. Write 2-3 variants (different hooks, different CTAs, different opening lines).
2. Send each to **at least 500-1000 prospects** so the data is statistically meaningful. Below 100 sends per variant the data is noise.
3. Measure the funnel: open rate, reply rate, opt-in rate, booked-call rate, proposal-sent rate, close rate, lifetime value of those closed.
4. Kill the bottom performers fast. Do not be sentimental about copy you wrote yesterday.
5. Generate new variants from the top performer (vary one thing at a time; if you change the hook *and* the CTA, you do not know which one moved the number).
6. Loop.

Most DivineSide campaigns will start at 2-4% reply rate and need 2-3 iteration cycles to climb to the 8-12% range. That climb is where the money is. See [§09](#09--iteration-discipline) for full iteration cadence.

**Stated vs revealed preference.** Trust what prospects *do*, not what they *say*. A prospect who replies "I love this messaging" but never books is a no. A prospect who replies "this is annoying" and books anyway is a yes. The market is the only true judge. Watch behavior, not commentary.

## 03 · The Four-Step Copy Framework

Every cold message is made of four parts in this order. Each part has a specific job, answering a specific question the reader is silently asking.

| Step | Question it answers in the reader's head |
|------|------------------------------------------|
| 1. Personalization | *"Is this a scammer or a spammer?"* |
| 2. Who am I | *"OK, who is this person and why should I care?"* |
| 3. Offer | *"What's actually in it for me?"* |
| 4. CTA | *"What do they want me to do, and is it worth it?"* |

You answer these in order because the reader asks them in order. Skipping or reordering means you answer a question they have not asked yet, and the message reads wrong.

### Step 1. Personalization (the opener)

**This is the single highest-ROI line in the entire message.** Email reader drop-off is brutal: if 100% read word one, 50% are still reading by word ten. The opener is the only line you can guarantee everyone sees, so it has to do the most work.

**Job of the opener:** make the reader stop and think *"wait, did this person actually look at me?"* That moment of hesitation buys you the next 30 seconds of attention. **You are sneakily evading their sales radar.** That is the actual job.

**Rules:**

- **Two sentences max. One ideal.** The longer the personalization, the less personal it reads. Long personalization is the #1 tell of an LLM-written cold email.
- **Specific over flattering.** "Saw your post on shipping the Q2 redesign in three weeks" beats "love how passionate you are about innovation." Flattery without a hook is AI-slop.
- **Pass the human test.** Would an actual human notice this thing? If the answer requires three logical leaps, no.
- **Segue into the pitch.** The personalization should naturally hand off to *why you are messaging.* If you have to abruptly pivot from "love your blog" to "here is what I sell," you wrote the wrong opener.

**Personalization formula:**

`[Greeting] + [Specific observation about THEM] + [Voluntary disclosure or segue]`

#### Cold Reading

Cold reading is the technique mentalists and psychics use: make a general statement that sounds personal but actually applies to ~80% of the population. The reader cannot tell because they think they are special.

Cold reading is the **default recommended strategy** for personalization. You do not even need AI to do it.

Examples:

- *"Hey Nick, love your channel, man. Very no-BS and helped me get started in management consulting."*. Works for any business YouTuber. Every YouTuber thinks their channel is no-BS. Every business is general enough that "got me started in management consulting" plausibly applies.
- *"Huge work on the [public revenue number] solo, man. We're lucky to have you."*. Works for any creator at scale.
- *"Been with you since the OG days, much-deserved traction."*. Sounds personal, applies to anyone with a following.

**Cold reading + scraped variable** is the next level up. Use AI to scrape one specific data point (school, neighborhood, a recent post title) and slot it into a cold-read template. Don't have AI write the whole line. (See [§11](#11--using-ai-in-copywriting).)

#### Voluntary Disclosure

Bake one piece of personal info about *yourself* into the opener. The reader trusts you more for having shared something. The disclosure does not need to be true in any meaningful sense; it just needs to be specific.

- *"It helped me get my start in management consulting."*
- *"My girlfriend says I listen to you more than I listen to her, lol."*
- *"You're a common lunch topic at the [Company] HQ, lol."*

#### DO Examples

- *"Hey Marcus. Saw the breakdown of how you cut your AOV calculation latency in half. The 'we just ran the SQL slower on purpose' line killed me. Made me want to send this..."*
- *"Hey. Spotted you guys are hiring two more SDRs in London. That is exactly the moment when the thing I am building usually pays for itself."*
- *"Hey Dan. Noticed your booking flow has the Calendly redirect on the *third* page. Curious if that was deliberate."*

#### DON'T Examples

- *"Hi Stacy! I was so impressed by your passion for process optimization and your commitment to fostering inclusive corporate cultures at BeaverCorp."*. Vague AI flattery. No human reads a LinkedIn profile and thinks "wow, process optimization, my kind of person."
- *"Hope this finds you well!"*. Zero personalization. Skip.
- *"Congrats on your work at [Company]!"*. Empty. Means nothing.
- *"I love what you guys are doing in the AI space."*. The reader's brain reads this as "I have not looked at you at all."
- *"Hi Nick Sar Daily Updates"*. Variable scrape failure. Worse than no greeting because it advertises the template.
- *"I watched your video 'Use this advanced Claude Code prompt to get 10x better results from your AI agent.'"* with quotes around the title. The quotes give away that the title was scraped and pasted.

### Step 2. Who Am I (one line, max two)

After the opener earns attention, the reader's next question is *"OK, who is this person and why are they writing to me?"* Answer it in one line. Two if absolutely necessary.

The reader is asking this because they have just confirmed you are not a scammer (the personalization did that work). Now they want to know if you matter.

**Rules:**

- **One line. Max two.** Rapid-fire pattern matching. They are not reading carefully yet.
- **Lead with the most credible thing the reader will recognize.** If they will not recognize the credential, do not use it.
- **Do not list everything you do.** Pick the one thing relevant to *this* prospect.
- **No company tagline.** Nobody cares.
- **Combine with social proof.** The strongest "who am I" line is *"I currently work with [client similar to prospect] to help them do [thing similar to what prospect needs]. We've done [specific number] in [specific timeframe]."*

When you say *"we work with [client like the prospect]"*, you are doing four things at once: introducing yourself, showing social proof, signaling in-group membership, and quietly flexing.

#### Pang/DivineSide Examples

- *"I run delivery at a small AIOS shop. We just shipped a build for a UK beauty brand doing the same thing you are."*
- *"Founder of DivineSide. We build internal AI ops systems for ecom brands 5-50 people."*
- *"I am Pang. I do the engineering on AI workflows for founder-led businesses."*

The "who am I" should set up the offer, not justify your existence.

### Step 3. The Offer (the value proposition)

What you are giving them and why it matters to *them specifically*. The offer is the "so what" of the message.

This is where most cold messages fail. They have a service to sell; they do not have an offer. The difference is huge. See [§04](#04--the-offer-formula) for the full offer formula. Short version of the rules:

- **Specific outcome, not service category.** "8 hours a week back by week 4" beats "AI automation services."
- **Quantified.** Numbers, time saved, dollars, percentages. Vague benefits get skimmed.
- **Time-bound.** *"In 60 days,"* not *"quickly."* Without a time bound, the guarantee in §04 cannot exist.
- **Risk-reversed.** Real guarantee. Real teeth. *"Or you don't pay."*
- **Tied to their context.** Reference the personalization. The reader should feel the offer was built for them, not pasted in.
- **Address one pain.** Multiple pains in one message dilutes everything. Pick the one you can prove you solve.

For DivineSide specifically the offer language defaults to outcome-led (*"your business, running on AI"*) per [`../sales-and-delivery/offer.md`](../sales-and-delivery/offer.md) §01. Reserve the AIOS category-noun for moments where the reader needs the framing to understand the differentiation.

### Step 4. The CTA

One ask. Specific. Frictionless.

**Rules:**

- **One CTA per message.** Two CTAs = the reader does neither.
- **Match the CTA to the goal you set in §02-A.** If the goal was a reply, ask a question. If the goal was a Loom view, link the Loom. If the goal was a call, propose two specific time slots in *their* timezone.
- **Lower the activation energy.** "Want me to send you the Loom?" (one-word reply) beats "Click here to book a 30-minute discovery session" (six steps).
- **Permission to decline.** Counter-intuitively, "tell me to screw off and I won't message again" usually *raises* reply rate. It removes the social cost of saying no, which means the social cost of saying yes also drops.
- **Eliminate back-and-forth steps.** Every step between message and conversion leaks ~5% of prospects. The standard call-booking sequence (you ask → they say yes → you ask time → they propose → you counter → they counter again) leaks ~25% of replies just to scheduling friction. Propose specific times in the first message.

#### DO CTAs

- *"Want me to record a 60-second Loom showing the exact fix? Just say 'yes' and it lands in your inbox today."*
- *"Worth a 15-min call this Thursday at 10 or 2 your time? If neither works, tell me what does. I can send a one-click Google Meet invite or ring you at the number on your site."*
- *"Reply with 'send' and I will share the spec doc."*

#### DON'T CTAs

- *"Let me know your thoughts!"*. Vague. Asks for nothing specific.
- *"Book a discovery call: [calendly link]"*. No reason given, full burden on the reader.
- *"Looking forward to hearing from you."*. Polite filler. Not a CTA.
- *"Would love to chat about how we can help you scale your business with AI-powered automation."*. Generic + multi-step + no time proposed.
- *"Let me know if you're interested."*. Pushes the next step entirely onto the reader.

## 04 · The Offer Formula

Most cold messages have a service to sell. The senior practitioners have an *offer*. This is the single biggest lever in cold outbound after personalization.

### The Equation

```
Conversion rate  =  ( ROI  ×  Trust ) / Friction
```

Three variables, every offer has all three:

- **ROI**: what return are you promising? Quantified, time-bound, specific. *"$10,000 in revenue in 60 days,"* not *"more sales."*
- **Trust**: how confident are they that you can deliver? Built by social proof, in-group references, authority signals, rapport. (Exists *outside* the offer line itself, in §03 step 2 and the body.)
- **Friction**: how hard is it for them to start? Number of steps, time required, money required, work required.

You are not literally calculating this. You use the formula to **make sure all three knobs are turned** in every message. If ROI is high but friction is high, conversion drops. If ROI and friction are perfect but trust is missing, the offer reads as too-good-to-be-true and conversion drops.

### Constructing the Offer Line

The offer is one sentence in this shape:

```
I will do [specific outcome] in [specific time], or [specific risk reversal].
```

Three slots:

1. **Outcome.** Quantified. Unambiguous. *"Book you 20 sales meetings"* not *"book you some meetings."*
2. **Time bound.** *"In 60 days."* Without a time bound, the guarantee cannot exist (the prospect can always say "I am still waiting").
3. **Risk reversal / guarantee.** *"Or you don't pay,"* or *"Or I work for free until I do,"* or *"I'll send you a $500 gift card to your favorite steak place."* The risk should sit on you, not the prospect.

### Why Big-Sounding Offers Are Not Crazy

When you offer *"$100K in revenue in 60 days,"* the prospect hears "that's wild." But check the math:

- A business doing $300K/mo profit makes $600K-$700K in 60 days.
- $100K of *revenue* (not profit) is ~16% lift on their funnel for the period.
- A real practitioner can find 16% lift in any business that has any inefficiencies.

**Offers scale with prospect size.** Pitch a $5M/yr YouTuber on $100K in 60 days = 2% lift. Pitch a $200K/yr local shop on $20K in 60 days = same percentage. Use the prospect's own revenue scale to set the offer; you are anchoring on a percentage you can actually move.

### The 3x Top-of-Funnel Rule

A guaranteed offer roughly **3x's top-of-funnel response** vs no offer. Some percentage of work will be unpaid (failed guarantees), but that's typically ~10% of margin lost in exchange for ~270% revenue gain. Net: ~2.7x more profit. The math always favors having an offer.

### Self-Liquidating Offers

A subset of offers where you do the work upfront for free, and only ask for payment if the work succeeds or the prospect signs on. You don't need a time bound because the offer is self-policing: you only get paid when the outcome lands.

- *"I'll build you a world-class live chat widget on your website at no cost. I won't charge you anything until you get your first 10 paying clients."*
- *"Send me three of your last email campaigns. I'll rewrite them for free and tell you exactly why the new versions will convert better. No strings."*
- *"I'll redesign your homepage for free. We send a mockup within 72 hours; you only pay if you absolutely love it."*

These work because the friction goes to zero (literally free) and the trust requirement also drops (you're not asking for any commitment).

### Worked Offer Examples

Each line below uses the same three slots: **outcome** (what they get) / **time** (by when) / **risk reversal** (what happens if you fail).

| # | Offer | Outcome | Time | Risk reversal |
|---|-------|---------|------|---------------|
| 1 | I will guarantee you 20 booked sales appointments in the next 60 days or you don't pay, no strings. Just say yes and I'll get started. | 20 booked appointments | 60 days | Don't pay |
| 2 | I'll build a world-class live chat widget on your website at no cost. I won't charge you anything until you get your first 10 paying clients. | Live chat widget + 10 paying clients | (self-liquidating) | Free until result |
| 3 | I will build you the same high-converting proposal template that's made [Company] $5M in the last 2 months. I'll do all the work upfront. Only if you like it will I ask you to work with me. Just say yes and I'll send yours within 48 hours. | High-converting proposal template | 48 hours | Pay only if you like it |
| 4 | I'll run a full SEO audit on your site and show you exactly which pages are losing you traffic, completely free. Just say yes; I'll have it in your inbox within 24 hours. | Full SEO audit | 24 hours | Free |
| 5 | I'll build you a Google Ads campaign that I guarantee will generate 50 qualified leads in 30 days, or you don't pay a cent. Just say yes and I'll get it live this week. | Google Ads campaign + 50 qualified leads | 30 days | Don't pay |
| 6 | Just send us a title and we'll give you a completely free 500-word blog post on the topic in 48 hours. No strings attached. | 500-word blog post | 48 hours | Free |
| 7 | I will build you a world-class industry CRM at no cost. I'll pay for it all myself, do all the work upfront, and only if you like it will I ask you to work with me. Just say yes and I'll send a link within 48 hours. | CRM build | 48 hours | Pay only if you like it |
| 8 | I'll give you completely free entry into [program] which lets you fulfill your 20 CME credit requirement at no cost. Just say yes and we'll send a private invite link. | Free entry + private invite link | (program-bound) | Free |
| 9 | I'll redesign your homepage for free. We send a mockup within 72 hours; you only pay if you get 5%+ higher conversion rate. | Homepage redesign | 72 hours | Pay only on 5%+ CRO win |

Notice that not every offer follows the exact same template. Some collapse outcome and risk-reversal into one slot (free offers). Some are self-liquidating with no explicit time. **The pattern is: outcome + time + reversed risk, in some combination.**

### System Beats Template

Templates work for a week or a month, then the market sees them and they stop working. **A system produces infinite templates.** If you understand the formula (the system), you can write any template; if you only have one template, you have nothing once it dies.

This is why this playbook teaches the system, not the templates. Anybody who hands you a copy-paste template has handed you something with a 90-day shelf life. The formula has a decade-plus shelf life.

## 05 · Goal-Specific Templates

Templates for the three CTA goals that matter most for DivineSide. Treat as scaffolding; rewrite each for the specific prospect. Do not paste verbatim.

### Template A. Reply Goal

```
Subject: [lowercase, ≤4 words, related to the personalization]

Hey [first name].

[1-sentence specific observation about them.]

[1-sentence "who I am" tied to the observation.]

[1-sentence offer or insight you'd give them if they replied.]

Want me to send the [thing]? Just say yes.

[First name]
Sent from my iPhone
```

### Template B. Loom / Asset Goal

```
Subject: [lowercase, references the asset]

Hey [first name].

[1-sentence specific observation about them.]

Recorded a 90-second walkthrough of [specific thing relevant to them]. Shows [exact outcome].

[Loom link]

If even 30 seconds in it's not useful, tell me to screw off. I won't message again.

[First name]
```

### Template C. Book a Call Goal

```
Subject: [lowercase, ≤4 words]

Hey [first name].

[1-sentence specific observation about them.]

I [credibility line]. Just shipped [matching social proof: 1 line, named client + numbers + timeframe].

I'd [specific outcome] in [specific time], or [risk reversal]. We'd only need to talk once for ~15 min; I'd handle everything else in the background.

How does [time slot 1, their timezone] sound? Could give you a ring at [phone if you have it] or send a one-click Google Meet invite. If neither works, [time slot 2].

[First name]
```

## 06 · Lessons from Live Rewrites

Distilled from 10 cold messages the source roasted and rewrote. These are the recurring failures and recurring fixes.

### Recurring Failures (real examples from rewrites)

| Failure | Real example seen | What was wrong |
|---------|-------------------|----------------|
| Variable scrape leaks the template | *"Hi Nick Sar Daily Updates"* (scraped YouTube channel name as if it were a last name) | Take the first word only; if it has a space, the space is usually the name boundary |
| Quoted scraped variables | *"I watched your video 'Use this advanced Claude Code prompt...'"* with quotes | Quotes signal the variable was pasted in. Strip quotes. |
| AI-system signature giveaways | *"Email tracked with MailSweet"* signature | Disable open-tracking, especially the kind that adds a signature |
| Self-identifying as AI | *"I'm Grove, an AI CEO running a 10-agent operations system"* | Branding yourself as a robot tanks reply rate. Use a human name. |
| Pricing in the email | *"$500 for a single agent starter, $25,500 for the full setup"* | Never put pricing in cold outbound. Sell the call, not the line item. |
| Links in the email | *"Click here: groveAI.example/setup"* + email + landing page link | Links tank deliverability (Gmail/Outlook flag for spam) and signal "scammer." Save links for the second message. |
| "We" instead of "I" | *"We support companies refine their value proposition, align marketing and sales handoffs..."* | Use "I." The reader is talking to a person, not a brand. |
| Title rephrased as buzzword salad | *"I support companies in refining their value proposition through aligned go-to-market motions..."* | Write at the reader's grade level, not consultant level. *"I help marketing companies fix their funnel"* beats the above. |
| Pretending to give without actually giving | *"Happy to show you over a casual 15-minute video call"* presented as the give | A 15-minute call is taking, not giving. Give means giving a Loom, an audit, a free mockup, a free template, free credits. |
| Opening with self-introduction | *"My name is Imran, I'm a thumbnail designer, I noticed..."* | Opener has to earn the read. Self-introduction goes in step 2, not step 1. |
| Long, illustrious signature | *"Kind regards, Peter Beller, Managing Partner at Morningside Advisors"* | Cut. *"Thanks, Peter"* is enough. |
| Obvious mass-template tells | *"To make a long story short, we've got around 1 million followers on multiple platforms..."* with no personalization | If line one doesn't pass the human test, nothing later in the email saves it. |

### Recurring Fixes

- Replace company-formal name with casualization. *"Pacific Creative Group LLC"* becomes *"PCG."* *"Vancouver, British Columbia"* becomes *"East Van."* The reader thinks "this person knows me."
- Replace generic flattery with cold-readable specificity. *"Your channel rocks"* becomes *"Your channel is no-BS, helped me get my start in management consulting"* (cold read + voluntary disclosure in one line).
- Replace vague offer with quantified offer + guarantee. *"We can help you scale"* becomes *"I'd guarantee you at least $50K in added top-line revenue or I'd work for free until I got you this 100% attributable."*
- Replace "let me know your thoughts" with two specific time slots. *"Worth 15 min? I have Tuesday at 10 or Thursday at 2 your time."*
- Replace mass-broadcast voice with one-to-one voice. *"We help companies like yours"* becomes *"I work with [specific named company similar to you] right now."*

## 07 · Channel-Specific Optimization

The four-step framework holds across channels. The constraints, optimizable surfaces, and tone change.

### Cold Email

The optimizable surfaces, in the order the reader sees them:

| Surface | Constraint | What to optimize |
|---------|-----------|------------------|
| Sender name | ~20 chars | Use a real first + last name. Short first names give more room downstream. |
| Subject line | ~30-50 chars | Lowercase, plausible-deniability framing (see [§08](#08--subject-lines)). |
| Teaser (preview) | ~150 chars combined with subject | Whatever you do not use in the subject becomes teaser space. Unused space gets filled with metadata (date, etc.) which looks like spam. Aim to use the full ~150 combined. |
| Profile picture | Square, recognizable | Smiling, decent lighting. The Jordan example: blue background + white backdrop = high contrast = stands out. |
| Sender email address | Visible in header | Use a real-looking domain. *"firstname@company.com"* beats *"firstname.lastname123@company.com"*. |
| Body | ~90 words ideal | Four-step framework. |

**Email-specific rule:** any unused space in subject + teaser gets auto-filled with date/sender metadata, which reads like spam. Always fill the full 150 characters.

### LinkedIn DM

| Surface | Constraint | What to optimize |
|---------|-----------|------------------|
| Profile picture | Square, professional | Smiling, professional, contrast background. Most-judged element on LinkedIn. |
| Full name | ~20 chars | Short first names = more teaser room. |
| Title (job role) | ~50-60 chars | Use this for credibility. *"AI Operating Systems @ DivineSide"* beats *"Founder @ DivineSide."* |
| Premium / partnership badge | Gold icon next to name | Get it. It signals authority and improves out-of-other-mailbox rate. Look for partner programs in your niche (HubSpot Elite, Google Partner, etc.). |
| Teaser | ~50-55 chars before truncation | First sentence of message. Hooks the open. |
| Body | ~60 words ideal | Four-step framework, slightly more compressed than email. |

**LinkedIn-specific rule:** main inbox vs "other" inbox is the key conversion gate. Profile completeness, premium status, and the badge significantly affect which one you land in.

### X DM

| Surface | Constraint | What to optimize |
|---------|-----------|------------------|
| Profile picture | Square | Recognizable; X is heavily image-driven. |
| Display name | Short | Real-looking. |
| Handle | After @ | Should match the display name closely. *"@nick_sar"* is fine; *"@user398472"* is dead. |
| Account join date | Visible in profile | Recent accounts get auto-filtered as spam. Aged accounts (1+ year) significantly outperform. |
| Teaser | ~40-55 chars | Similar to LinkedIn but tighter. |
| Body | ~40 words ideal | Lowercase. Casual. Read like a tweet, not an email. |

**X-specific rule:** the whole game is escaping the **message requests** folder. If you're in requests, you're nearly invisible. Aged accounts + casual tone-mirror help.

### Instagram DM

| Surface | Constraint | What to optimize |
|---------|-----------|------------------|
| Profile picture | Round | Recognizable; voice/identity-led platform. |
| Handle / display name | ~30 chars before truncation | Real-looking, not algorithmic ("creditboost777" = dead). |
| Teaser | ~30 chars | Tighter than X. Very little space. |
| Body | ~40 words ideal | Lowercase, voice-driven, reads like a chat. |

**Instagram-specific rule:** also gated by the requests folder. Same fix as X: aged account + casual tone.

### iMessage / SMS

| Surface | Constraint | What to optimize |
|---------|-----------|------------------|
| Sender phone number | Visible | iMessage (blue) significantly outperforms SMS (green). |
| Message teaser | ~90 chars before truncation | The whole message is often visible without opening. Bury something interesting in the *last* part of the teaser to force a click. |
| Body | ~25 words ideal | Single ask, single line of context, single CTA. |

**SMS-specific rule:** real humans send SMS short. Long SMS reads as bot.

### Cross-Channel Rule

The more conversational the channel, the harder you have to compress and the more **implicit rapport** (form mirroring) matters. An IG DM that reads like an email gets read as spam.

## 08 · Subject Lines

The subject line has one job and one job only: **buy the click via plausible deniability.**

### The Plausible Deniability Principle

The reader's first question on every cold email is: *"Is this spam, or might this be someone who knows me?"* A great subject line keeps them uncertain just long enough to open.

You **never sell in the subject line**. Selling = the reader has answered the question without opening the email = no open. The subject line gives just enough to pique curiosity, not enough to satisfy.

The reader should be able to construct multiple possible explanations for the email: maybe an old friend, maybe a long-lost cousin, maybe someone who watched your content, maybe an investor, maybe a buyer, maybe a podcast invite. **You want all of those open as possibilities.**

### Loss-Framed Beats Gain-Framed

Humans respond more strongly to loss than to gain. *"You're wasting $2,300/month"* outperforms *"You could earn $2,300/month more."* If your offer can be reframed as preventing a loss, do it in the subject.

### Length

~30-50 characters. Short enough to not get truncated. Short enough to leave room for the teaser to do its own work.

### Capitalization

Lowercase signals "real human, typed quickly on a phone." Title Case Signals Press Release. Default to lowercase unless the persona's voice file says otherwise.

### DO Examples

- *"your claude code course and a formatting thought"*: lowercase, references something only a viewer would know, hints at value (a thought) without giving it
- *"2026 capital"*: could mean anything; reader has to open to find out
- *"been with you from the start"*: nobody writes "start" that way, hints at long history
- *"nick + youtube"*: short, ambiguous, name plus context
- *"nick's blind spot"*: fear-of-loss + curiosity
- *"nick, you're wasting $2,300 per month"*: loss framing + specific number + lowercase mistake-look
- *"are you taking coaching clients?"*: could be a real prospect
- *"are you hiring?"*: could be a real candidate
- *"you looked sad in your last vid"*: clearly someone who watched
- *"quick q"* / *"nick q"*: minimal, friend-like

### DON'T Examples

- *"Quick collab?"*: generic, no personalization, screams template
- *"Video editor"*: pure category label, signals service pitch
- *"Nick, you scaled to 72K a month with automation. Here's the next level."*: sells the whole pitch in the subject; nothing left to discover
- *"Interested in AI driven performance optimization"*: the fact that AI is two words proves the sender doesn't actually work in AI (anyone in AI writes "AI"). Wrong-shibboleth tells.
- *(no subject)*: correlated with spam; never send empty
- *"Loved your fastcast strategy"*: too vague, reads like AI
- *"Paid Collaboration"*: generic, capitalizes both words like a press release
- *"COULD YOU SEND?"*: all caps reads as scam

## 09 · Iteration Discipline

Almost no campaign works on the first send. Iteration is what gets a campaign from 2.5% to 10% reply rate. Most senders skip this entirely.

### Always Run Multiple Variants

Single-variant campaigns are leaving money on the table by definition. Most outbound platforms (Instantly, Smartlead, Heyreach, etc.) support A/B variants natively. Use them.

### Stick to a Schedule

The single best operational rule: **iterate every Sunday for 20-30 min.** Dedicated, recurring, low-effort. Most people iterate intensely for a week, then forget. Sunday-iteration discipline gets you ~50 cycles/year instead of ~5.

### 500-1000 Per Variant for Statistical Significance

Below 500 sends per variant, the data is noise. Resist the temptation to declare a winner after 100 sends. A campaign that got 0 replies on 100 sends is not statistically distinguishable from a campaign that got 1.

### Pick a TAM with Enough Leads

If your total addressable market is 200 prospects, you can't iterate. You barely have one statistically meaningful test. **Default to TAMs of 10,000+** so you have room for ~20 iteration cycles.

### Big Changes Early, Small Changes Late

Iteration arc:

| Cycle | Type of change |
|-------|----------------|
| Cycles 1-2 | **Fundamentally different variants.** Short vs long. Formal vs casual. Personalized vs cold-read-only. ~3x difference in shape. Goal: explore the search space wide. |
| Cycles 3-4 | **Substantially different variants.** Different hook, different CTA, different offer angle. ~2x difference. |
| Cycles 5-6 | **Modestly different variants.** Different opening line, different social proof. ~1x difference. |
| Cycles 7+ | **Tweaks.** Word changes, subject line A/B, time-of-send. ~0.5x difference. |

You explore wide first because you don't know where the peak is. Once you've found the rough peak (highest-performing shape), you narrow in with smaller and smaller changes.

### Kill the Bottom Performers Fast

If campaign A is at 10% reply rate and B and C are at 2%, do not keep sending B and C "for variety." Kill them, build new variants from A.

### Trust the Market, Not Your Gut

Intuition about what will work is consistently worse than the data. Plenty of campaigns that "felt off" hit 15% reply rates. Plenty that "felt killer" got 1%. Run them all.

## 10 · Follow-Ups

Most senders either don't follow up at all, or follow up too aggressively with newsletter-style content. Both fail.

### Start with Two Messages, Not Five

Initial sequence: **one initial + one follow-up.** That's it. Test the sequence, measure reply rate, then add a third only after the two-message sequence is proven to perform.

Why: every additional follow-up raises spam-mark / block / archive risk. If your initial copy is bad, sending five follow-ups just amplifies how much the prospect dislikes you. Prove the copy works on a 2-message sequence first, then add steps to amplify a winner.

When to add a 3rd: if your 2-message sequence is at 4-5%+ reply rate. The 3rd message will typically lift it ~1-1.5pp (e.g., 4.8% → 6.1%).

### Follow-Ups Should Sound Human

The biggest mistake: writing follow-ups like newsletters. *"Peter, while analyzing LeftClick I noticed you were spending $X on platforms you didn't have to be. Our recent case study with AAA Corp saved over $10,000..."*. Nobody talks like this in real life.

Real human follow-ups:

- *"Hey Pete, you cool man? Get back to me, let me know how this is."*
- *"Hey Pete, just checking in on X. Let me know if you have any questions."*
- *"Hey Pete, did this get lost in your inbox?"*
- *"Bumping this to the top in case you missed it."*

That's it. Short. Casual. Like you actually know them.

### Vary the Subject Line

Each follow-up gets a different subject line. This is also a free A/B test on the subject: if the second subject pulls more opens than the first, you've learned something.

## 11 · Using AI in Copywriting

The honest answer from someone who runs an AI community of 10,000+ people doing this professionally: **don't use AI to write your whole email.** AI is also not yet good enough to meaningfully craft persuasive copy. People who hand the whole email to an LLM tend to suck.

### The Skill Curve

Cold copywriting has a fast learning curve up to ~75% competence (a few weeks of focused practice gets you most of the way). The remaining 25% takes years. **AI gets you to ~75%.** That last 25% is where the actual money is, and AI cannot reach it.

The market floor for "good enough copy that gets opened at all" is now *above* what AI alone can produce. So if you let AI write everything, you don't even clear the floor.

### What to Use AI For

#### 1. Templated Variables Inside a Working Template

Don't have AI write the email. Have AI fill *one slot* inside a template you wrote. Examples:

- "Generate a one-line cold-read opener referencing this person's most popular content type."
- "Pick a phrase like *'love your channel, man'* if YouTube, *'love your LinkedIn posts, man'* if LinkedIn, *'big fan of your work on [topic]'* if both."

The rest of the email is hand-written. Only the variable slot is AI.

#### 2. Casualization Layer

This is the highest-leverage AI use in cold outbound. Take scraped variables and make them sound like a human said them.

- Company name: *"Pacific Creative Group LLC"* → *"PCG"*
- City: *"Vancouver, British Columbia"* → *"East Van"*
- School: *"University of California, Los Angeles"* → *"UCLA"*

The reader thinks: *"only someone who actually knows the area / company / school would write it this way."* Builds trust without you doing any work.

This same pattern applies to neighborhoods, sports teams, slang names for industries, etc. AI tends to have enough cultural knowledge to do these casualizations across most major regions.

#### 3. Lead Scraping and Enrichment

Use AI to find and enrich prospects (extract company size, recent posts, hiring signals). This is upstream of writing and doesn't touch the copy itself. Largely safe.

### What NOT to Use AI For

- Writing the whole message
- Writing the personalization line in full (use it to *fill a variable* in a hand-written template, not to compose the line)
- Generating offers (the offer formula has to be reasoned through, not generated)
- Writing follow-ups (these are the easiest part to write yourself; AI ones read as bot)

## 12 · Grey-Hat Techniques (informational only)

These are techniques that trade compliance with platform ToS or local regulations for outbound leverage. **Documented for awareness, not endorsed.** Account suspension and legal exposure are real risks. DivineSide does not recommend any of these as default practice.

### Pre-Warmed Mailboxes / Accounts

The market sells aged email mailboxes, LinkedIn accounts, X accounts, etc. that have been warmed up over weeks or months. Higher send limits, less likely to be flagged. Most major outbound platforms (Instantly, Smartlead, etc.) sell pre-warmed mailboxes natively.

Risk: if a prospect asks who *"Stacy at 123click.com"* is when you're really *"Pang at oneboxagency.com,"* you have to explain. Some teams pass the persona off as a secretary.

### Power Dialers

For cold calling. Parallelizes outbound dialing across multiple lines so you connect to whoever picks up first. ~2.5x utilization. Some platforms do automatic voicemail drops (record once, drop to thousands), which is heavily regulated in many jurisdictions.

### Cold SMS / WhatsApp via Third-Party APIs

The grey-hat-iest of the three. Cold SMS in most markets is heavily regulated; WhatsApp business outreach is technically restricted. Third-party APIs exist that distribute volume across many numbers in parallel to fly under detection. Some emulate iMessage blue-bubble sending behavior to raise reply rates.

Legality varies sharply by jurisdiction. Default position: **don't.**

## 13 · The Pre-Send Checklist

Before any cold message goes out, run it through this:

1. **Goal.** Can I describe what I want the reader to do in one sentence? Does the message ask for that one thing and only that thing?
2. **Frame.** If a friend read this over my shoulder, would they think it was a personal message?
3. **Personalization.** Is line one specific enough that the reader will pause? Would a human notice this thing? Cold-read or scraped specific (not vague flattery)?
4. **Voluntary disclosure.** Is there one piece of personal info I'm sharing about myself?
5. **Length.** Under the channel cap? (Email <90 words. LinkedIn <60. IG/X <40. SMS <25.)
6. **Principles.** Did I bake in at least three of the seven (give first, micro commitment, social proof, authority, rapport, voluntary disclosure, scarcity, shared identity)?
7. **Offer.** Is there a quantified outcome + time bound + risk reversal?
8. **CTA.** One ask, specific, low-friction, two specific time slots if booking? Escape hatch included?
9. **Forbidden list.** Did I scrub all the corporate signals?
10. **Voice.** Does it sound like the persona file? Em-dashes scrubbed?
11. **AI tells.** Does it sound like a human typed it on a phone, or like an LLM filled a template? Are scraped variables casualized?
12. **Subject + teaser.** Subject lowercase, plausible-deniable? Teaser uses the available ~150 chars?
13. **Variant.** Am I testing this against another variant, or am I single-shotting?

If all 13 pass, send. If any fails, rewrite that specific part.

## 14 · The Forbidden List

If any of these appear in a message, rewrite it.

- *"Hope this finds you well"* (and all variants)
- *"I trust you are doing well"*
- *"Reaching out to you regarding..."*
- *"My name is [X] and I am the [title] at [company]"* as the opening line
- *"Just wanted to..."* / *"Just checking in"*
- *"Touch base"*
- *"Circle back"*
- *"At your earliest convenience"*
- *"Synergy"* / *"leverage"* (verb) / *"unlock"* / *"transform"*
- *"Game-changer"*
- Multi-paragraph signature blocks
- *"Best regards,"* / *"Warm regards,"* / any flowery sign-off
- Generic Calendly link with no one-line reason
- *"Looking forward to hearing from you"* as the closing line
- Em-dashes (—). Period, comma, parens, or colon instead. (See [`../identity/voice.md`](../identity/voice.md).)
- *"What do you think?"* as the only CTA
- *"Hope you are having a great week!"* anywhere
- Quotes around scraped variables (*"I watched your video '...'"*): quotes give away the template
- Capitalized acronyms wrong for the niche (e.g., *"AI"* as two words to AI people)
- Self-identifying as AI / a bot
- Pricing in the cold message
- Links in the cold message body (especially the first message)
- "We" when you mean "I"

## 16 · Running a Campaign. Operational SOP

The mechanics of actually shipping and managing a campaign from launch to iteration. DivineSide-specific (assumes the Instantly + Supabase + Discord pipeline at [`../../sales_os/integrations/instantly/`](../../../sales_os/integrations/instantly/)).

### A. Pre-Flight Checklist (before pressing launch)

1. **Test send to yourself.** `{{firstName}}` rendering correctly? Currency consistent throughout (no $/£ mix)? Em-dashes scrubbed? Subject lowercase? Body reads like a human typed it on a phone?
2. **Tracking off.** Turn off open/click tracking in Instantly. Tracking pixels add a fingerprint that can trip filters and reads as campaign-y. (`MailSweet` signature was the failure mode in the §06 rewrites.)
3. **Throttle per mailbox.** Cap each sending mailbox at 30-50 emails/day. Spread sends across the *reader's* business hours (9am-5pm UK time), not yours. Bulk-firing 360 at 9am sharp = campaign signal. Trickle = human signal.
4. **Domain warmup status.** Verify in Instantly that the sending mailboxes are warmed. New mailboxes need 2-3 weeks of warmup before scale sends.
5. **SPF / DKIM / DMARC.** Set on the sending domain. Instantly's onboarding usually catches this; if not, fix before scaling.
6. **List dedupe + suppression.** Confirm leads list is deduplicated and suppressed against any do-not-contact list or previous-campaign reply list.
7. **Positioning set in Supabase.** After the first daily poll lands the campaign row, set `outreach_campaigns.positioning` manually so the dashboard renders correctly (see [`../../sales_os/integrations/instantly/.overview.md`](../../../sales_os/integrations/instantly/.overview.md)).

### B. Launch Timing

| Day | UK send quality |
|---|---|
| Monday morning | Strong (peak fresh inbox) |
| Tuesday morning | **Best** (highest reply rates in B2B) |
| Wednesday morning | Strong |
| Thursday morning | Strong |
| Friday morning | OK |
| Friday afternoon / weekend | Dead |

For a 2-message sequence with a 3-day gap, **launch Monday or Tuesday morning UK time** so the follow-up lands Thursday or Friday (still a weekday). Avoid Friday launches: the follow-up lands Monday, which is fine, but Day 0 fights the weekend triage drift.

### C. Days 0-5. Hands Off

After launch, **don't touch the campaign for 5 days.** Hard rule. You'll want to tweak the copy after 24 hours of data; resist. Single-day reply rates are noise.

Watch only three things during this window:

- **Delivery rate.** Should be 95%+. Under 85% = deliverability problem (domain reputation, content triggers, warmup incomplete). Pause and fix; don't keep sending into a wall.
- **Discord positive-reply pings.** Real signal in real time, no aggregation needed.
- **Unsubscribe + complaint rate.** If complaints spike (>0.3%), copy is hitting the wrong inboxes or reading as spam to ICP. Pause and reconsider.

What *not* to watch: hourly reply rate, individual replies, your gut feel about whether it's working. None of those tell you anything statistically meaningful in the first 5 days.

### D. The Sunday Iteration Loop (Day 6 onward)

The single most important operational habit in cold outbound. 20-30 minutes every Sunday.

**Steps:**

1. **Pull the week's data** from the `/outreach/{campaign_id}` dashboard. Per-step reply rate, total sends, recent replies feed.
2. **Read every reply.** Categorize: positive / neutral / negative / wrong-person / OOO-already-filtered. Note patterns: is the positive set concentrated in a specific brand size, geography, role?
3. **Diagnose** against the threshold table below.
4. **Pick one change** for the next variant. First cycles: big change (different hook entirely, different subject framing). Later cycles: small change (one word, one line).
5. **Write the new variant.** Don't kill the current winner; stage the new variant alongside it.
6. **Log the change** somewhere persistent: what you changed, why, hypothesis for what should move. Markdown file or Notion row. Future-you needs this.

### E. Reply Rate Thresholds

Reply rate = **human replies only** (OOO and auto-replies are filtered at the integration layer; see [`../../sales_os/integrations/instantly/categories.py`](../../../sales_os/integrations/instantly/categories.py)).

| Rate | Diagnosis | Action |
|---|---|---|
| < 2% | Copy is broken | Kill the variant. Rewrite from scratch with a fundamentally different shape (different angle, different offer framing, different opener). |
| 2-4% | Baseline working | Add a 3x-different B variant for the next cycle. Don't tweak the current copy yet. |
| 5-7% | Strong | Add the break-up 3rd email (§10). Start scaling volume. |
| 8%+ | Crush | Don't change the copy. Push more volume into the same campaign. Look for whether you can expand the ICP without diluting. |

### F. Pacing Math

For a 2-message sequence with a 3-day gap:

- **Daily volume = initials + follow-ups.** In steady state, half your daily send cap goes to new leads (initials), half to leads from 3 days ago (follow-ups).
- **At 360 sends/day**, that's ~180 new leads/day. ~900 leads over 5 working days. Hits the 500-1000-per-variant stat-sig threshold in **5-6 days for a single variant**, ~10-12 days for a 2-variant A/B (split 90/day per variant).
- **Sunday review cadence** ([§09](#09--iteration-discipline)) means: launch Monday, first real data review the following Sunday (Day 6, ~1,080 leads in). Clean.

### G. OOO and Auto-Replies. Already Filtered

The Instantly integration drops these at the processor before they hit Supabase or Discord. They are not in your reply count. They are not in your dashboard. They are inbox activity, not a metric.

Do not measure against gross reply counts including OOO. The system measures correctly by default; don't override it.

### H. What Counts as a "Reply" That Matters

In order of value:

1. **Booked call.** The goal. Track in Instantly + Supabase.
2. **Positive reply.** Interested, wants more info, asks a clarifying question. Pinged in `#sales-and-outreach` → `outreach-replies` thread on Discord.
3. **Neutral reply.** Acknowledges but doesn't commit ("send me the details"). Counts toward reply rate, doesn't count toward conversion.
4. **Negative reply.** Not interested, wrong role, unsubscribe. Counts toward reply rate. Categorize the reasons: they tell you what to change.
5. **Wrong-person reply.** Forwards to the right person, says "this is my colleague's domain." Sometimes converts; treat as a positive lead even if not a positive *reply* on the original recipient.
6. **OOO / auto-reply.** Already filtered, not counted.

### I. When to Add a Third Email

Only after the 2-message sequence shows ≥5% reply rate over ≥1,000 sends. Adding a 3rd message earlier means more spam-mark risk, no real lift, and dirtier attribution (you can't tell which message is converting).

When you do add it: real reason to exist (genuine deadline, new piece of value, or break-up). Never template-shape. See [§10](#10--follow-ups).
